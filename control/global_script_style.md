# History Forge Global Script Style

## Brand Voice

- Write with a voice that is warm, intelligent, cinematic, slightly conversational, and recognizably human.
- The narration should feel like a thoughtful guide leading the audience through history, not a detached encyclopedia entry.
- Favor clear storytelling over dense explanation.
- Use language that sounds natural when spoken aloud.
- Maintain confidence without becoming grandiose or over-polished.

## Tone

- Keep the tone immersive and story-driven.
- Stay emotionally grounded, especially when describing conflict, loss, power, belief, or human consequence.
- Make the story clear and accessible for a broad audience without talking down to the viewer.
- Avoid sounding academic, sterile, overly formal, or mechanically summarized.
- The narration should feel like a trusted narrator guiding the audience through history.
- Use cinematic tension where appropriate, but keep the factual posture responsible.
- Let mystery, stakes, and consequence emerge from the material instead of forcing melodrama.

## Narrative Structure

- Open with a strong hook near the beginning.
- Build curiosity early by introducing a question, conflict, contradiction, surprising fact, or dramatic pressure point.
- Use transitions that keep momentum from one idea to the next.
- Avoid list-like delivery unless the user explicitly requests a list, countdown, or segmented format.
- Every section should move the story forward.
- Establish the historical setting quickly enough that viewers understand the stakes.
- Keep cause and effect clear: decisions, pressures, consequences, and turning points should connect naturally.
- End with a conclusion that gives the story meaning, not just a final fact.

## Authenticity Rules

- Acknowledge uncertainty where history is debated.
- Do not present speculation as settled fact.
- When possible, balance mystery with evidence.
- Avoid exaggeration unless it is clearly framed as storytelling language.
- Prefer careful phrasing such as "may have," "appears to," "some accounts suggest," or "historians debate" when certainty is limited.
- Do not invent precise details, dialogue, motives, dates, or personal thoughts unless the request clearly permits dramatization.
- Keep reconstructions plausible and grounded in the known era, culture, and setting.
- If a claim is controversial, signal that uncertainty rather than smoothing it away.

## Engagement Rules

- Use rhetorical questions sparingly and only when they sharpen curiosity.
- Use tension, curiosity, and reveal structure to maintain attention.
- Avoid repetitive phrasing across paragraphs or scenes.
- Avoid generic filler lines such as "history would never be the same" unless the script explains why.
- Prefer specific images, stakes, and consequences over vague excitement.
- Use short punchy lines for emphasis, but do not overuse them.
- Vary pacing so the script has moments of setup, pressure, reveal, and reflection.

## Short-Form Retention Rules

- For YouTube Shorts and other sub-90-second formats, the opening line should create immediate intrigue, tension, contrast, or surprise.
- Front-load the most arresting narrative pressure early rather than spending several lines warming up.
- Avoid soft intros such as "Today we're going to explore," "Let's talk about," or other low-tension openings.
- Each 1 to 2 sentences should either escalate the stakes, reveal a new detail, sharpen the central mystery, or move the audience toward a payoff.
- Use concise spoken rhythm that feels energetic without becoming breathless or sensational.
- Favor strong cause-and-effect storytelling over stacked facts.
- Build toward a memorable final line that lands on consequence, irony, scale, or human cost.
- Keep retention high through clarity, compression, and narrative momentum rather than clickbait phrasing.
- Do not overpromise, distort, or sensationalize the history just to create shock.

## CTA Style

- Calls to action should feel natural and brand-consistent.
- Never make the CTA overly aggressive, salesy, or out of tone.
- Fit the History Crossroads documentary tone: calm, confident, and connected to curiosity about the past.
- Keep CTAs brief unless the requested format requires a longer closing.
- A CTA should feel like an invitation to continue exploring history, not an interruption.

## Writing Constraints

- Avoid robotic phrasing.
- Avoid repeated sentence openings.
- Vary sentence length to create a natural spoken rhythm.
- Maintain clarity over complexity.
- Prefer vivid but controlled language.
- Avoid unnecessary jargon, academic throat-clearing, and over-explaining.
- Keep narration clean enough for text-to-speech and voiceover production.
- Do not include production notes, source notes, markdown fences, or side commentary unless specifically requested.
- Preserve factual caution while still making the story compelling.

## Summary Enforcement Rule

- Treat this file as the global writing standard for History Forge script outputs. Apply it to long-form narration and related script-generation workflows unless a more specific control file or explicit user override takes priority.
