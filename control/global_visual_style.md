# History Forge Global Visual Style

## Core Visual Identity

- Visuals should be cinematic, photorealistic, immersive, historically grounded, and dramatic but believable.
- Each image or video prompt should feel like a frame from a serious historical documentary.
- Prioritize credible historical realism over spectacle.
- Keep the visual world cohesive across scenes, with consistent era logic, materials, atmosphere, and tone.
- Avoid generic AI fantasy aesthetics unless the user explicitly requests stylization outside the History Crossroads brand.

## Lighting

- Use dramatic contrast where appropriate, especially for moments of conflict, secrecy, ritual, discovery, or consequence.
- Favor naturalistic lighting sources tied to the setting.
- Use golden hour, torchlight, candlelight, overcast skies, window light, firelight, moonlight, or practical interior light as appropriate.
- Avoid flat or artificial lighting unless the scene is intentionally modern.
- Lighting should support the emotional mood without hiding important historical details.
- Keep light direction and atmosphere consistent across adjacent scenes when they occur in the same time and place.

## Composition

- Build a strong sense of depth using foreground, midground, and background layering.
- Maintain a visually clear focal subject.
- Compose scenes like film frames, with deliberate subject placement and readable silhouettes.
- Use architecture, landscape, props, and crowds to support the story, not distract from it.
- Avoid clutter unless chaos, battle, panic, or dense urban life is intentional.
- Make the main action immediately understandable from the frame.
- Prefer believable camera language: wide establishing frames, medium documentary shots, controlled close-ups, and detail shots when useful.

## Historical Grounding

- Architecture, clothing, props, weapons, tools, transportation, and environments should feel era-appropriate.
- Avoid fantasy drift.
- Avoid anachronistic materials, technology, symbols, signage, or objects unless explicitly requested.
- Prioritize believable realism over spectacle.
- When historical evidence is uncertain, use plausible reconstruction rather than modern invention.
- Reflect regional differences in clothing, architecture, landscape, and material culture.
- Do not add readable text, modern logos, subtitles, watermarks, or interface-like elements to generated imagery.

## Texture and Atmosphere

- Real materials should feel tactile.
- Dust, smoke, weather, stone, wood, cloth, leather, bronze, iron, parchment, glass, and ceramic surfaces should read naturally.
- Atmosphere should enhance mood without obscuring the main subject.
- Use environmental details to create immersion: worn thresholds, rough stone, soot, fabric weave, patina, mud, rain, ash, mist, or sunlight through dust.
- Avoid overly clean environments unless historical context supports them.
- Keep skin, clothing, and materials natural rather than plastic, waxy, or polished.

## Motion and Cinematic Language

- If prompts involve motion, default to subtle cinematic motion.
- Favor slow push-in, pan, drift, reveal, parallax, controlled tilt, or a locked dramatic frame.
- Keep camera motion motivated by story emotion or discovery.
- Avoid chaotic camera language unless the scene explicitly requires panic, battle, collapse, or confusion.
- Maintain subject identity, anatomy, wardrobe, and setting continuity across motion prompts.
- Motion should reveal a new detail or deepen the moment rather than simply animate for its own sake.

## Avoid

- Cartoonish style.
- Plastic-looking skin or materials.
- Oversaturated colors.
- Generic AI fantasy aesthetics.
- Distorted anatomy.
- Overly glossy environments.
- Modern objects, modern lighting rigs, modern signage, or readable text unless explicitly requested.
- Random costumes, weapons, architecture, or props from the wrong era.
- Excessive visual clutter that makes the story action unclear.
- Hyper-stylized spectacle that undermines historical credibility.
